description: ABSTRACT. Dryland ecosystems are one of the most important ecosystems due to covering more than 40% of the Earth’s surface and feed 2 billion people. Dryland mixed farming system is typically integrated with rotational cropping and grazing. In this study, we quantified the diurnal, seasonal and interannual carbon and water fluxes in a typical dryland rotational cropping system in Southwest Western Australia using eddy covariance and remote sensing measurements. During the 9-year period (2016 to 2024), the ecosystem showed it was a net carbon source in 5 years and near carbon neutral or a carbon sink in 4 years. The annual evapotranspiration (ET) ranged from 234.67 to 421.49 mm year-1. Key meteorological drivers for gross primary productivity (GPP) were identified using a random forest analysis and showed variability across the growing season. Soil moisture was most important in the early growing season, solar radiation and vapour pressure deficit were significant in peak growing season and temperature became important in late growing season. Normalised difference vegetation index (NDVI) obtained using Sentinel 2 and Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) data showed that the finer spatial resolution of NDVI was more strongly correlated with GPP, and non-linear regression improved the prediction of GPP. Understanding the dryland agricultural system productivity and water dynamics are crucial for better resources management and ensuring food security. This study contributed to the carbon and water research in agricultural systems in Western Australia, as well as improved understanding of crop productivity at different growing stages at the national and international scale.
